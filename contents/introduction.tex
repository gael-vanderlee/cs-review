Virtual Reality (VR) technology has permeated numerous sectors beyond entertainment, becoming integral tools in fields such as healthcare, professional training, and education \cite{chatainGraspingDerivativesTeaching2022}. 
Generally, Virtual Reality (VR) is defined as a computer-generated simulation that enables users to perceive and interact with a three-dimensional environment through real-time multisensory feedback. 
A typical VR system includes a head-mounted display for stereoscopic rendering, motion-tracked controllers or sensors for user input, and a computing unit for environment simulation and rendering. However, there is no unifying definition of VR \cite{kardong-edgrenCallUnifyDefinitions2019}.

The successful application of VR thus hinges on its ability to deliver high-fidelity, convincing experiences.
Despite this potential, a significant barrier to widespread adoption is the onset of cybersickness (CS) \cite{casermanCybersicknessCurrentgenerationVirtual2021}.
Cybersickness is a specific form of visually induced motion sickness (VIMS) \cite{weechPresenceCybersicknessVirtual2019} characterized by symptoms such as nausea, dizziness, and disorientation that arise during or after immersion in a virtual environment \cite{casermanCybersicknessCurrentgenerationVirtual2021}.
However, the same set of symptoms is also observed in related conditions such as motion sickness (MS) and simulator sickness (SS). 
While distinctions have been proposed, such as CS being more strongly associated with compelling self-motion (a phenomenon known as vection) and disorientation, in contrast to the oculomotor or nausea-dominant profiles of SS and MS \cite{rebenitschReviewCybersicknessApplications2016}, those distinctions are not always taken into account in the literature.
Since these terms are often used interchangeably, this review considers findings across studies referring to Cybersickness, Visually Induced Motion Sickness, Simulator Sickness, and Motion Sickness, where relevant.

CS affects a substantial portion of the user population, with some studies reporting that 60\% to 95\% of participants experience some level of CS \cite{casermanCybersicknessCurrentgenerationVirtual2021}.
The prevalence of CS severely degrades the user experience, impairs task performance, and can lead to premature session termination \cite{mimnaughVirtualRealitySickness2023}.
The assessment of CS relies heavily on subjective, post-hoc measures like the Simulator Sickness Questionnaire (SSQ) \cite{kennedySimulatorSicknessQuestionnaire1993}.
However, such questionnaires are retrospective, prone to recall bias, and may not capture the dynamic nature of the sickness experience \cite{changBrainActivityCybersickness2023, dennisonImprovingMotionSickness2019}.
This reliance on subjective reports creates a critical need for objective, real-time metrics to accurately track the user's state.

Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCIs) present a promising methodology for addressing this challenge.
A BCI is a system that measures central nervous system activity and converts it into an artificial output that can replace, restore, enhance, supplement, or improve natural central nervous system output \cite{wolpawjonathanrBraincomputerInterfacesPrinciples2012}.
Within BCI research, a distinction is made between active, reactive, and passive systems \cite{wolpawjonathanrBraincomputerInterfacesPrinciples2012}.
Of particular relevance to CS research are passive BCIs, which utilize brain activity as an implicit source of information about the user's cognitive or affective state without the need for voluntary control.
By continuously monitoring neurophysiological signals such as the electroencephalogram (EEG), passive BCIs can provide an objective, real-time window into the user's internal experience.
This capability enables the development of a new class of user-aware adaptive systems that can respond dynamically to mitigate discomfort \cite{uyanCDMSRealtimeSystem2024, yamamuraHemodynamicVRAdaptingUsers2021a}.

This paper provides a systematic literature review and critical synthesis of the research at the intersection of BCI and cybersickness.
The primary aims of this review are threefold.
First, we synthesize the current state of the art, consolidating findings related to the neural correlates, prediction, and mitigation of CS using BCI methods.
Second, we critically appraise the methodologies, experimental designs, and analytical techniques prevalent in the existing literature, highlighting significant challenges to generalizability and replicability.
Then, we identify research gaps, and finally highlight potential future effective BCI-driven applications for managing cybersickness.
