Cybersickness remains a barrier to widespread virtual reality (VR) adoption. Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCI) and neuroimaging techniques offer promising means to objectively measure, predict, and mitigate this condition. 
This systematic review synthesizes current knowledge at the BCI-cybersickness intersection, identifying methodological challenges and proposing future research directions.
A systematic literature search across PubMed, Web of Science, and Scopus identified 83 peer-reviewed studies following inclusion criteria. 
Studies were categorized and compared based on objectives, neuroimaging modalities, computational methods, and findings.
Our synthesis highlights common neurophysiological signatures of cybersickness, with increased low-frequency (delta, theta) and decreased alpha-band power in EEG, and engagement of a cortico-subcortical network including the insula, cingulate cortex, and visual-vestibular regions in fMRI and fNIRS. 
Computational models, particularly deep learning approaches, demonstrate promise in classifying cybersickness states. Key research directions include predicting individual susceptibility from resting-state brain activity and developing closed-loop BCI systems that actively mitigate symptoms by modulating the VR environment in real-time.
While the field has transitioned from correlational studies to predictive models, progress is hampered by methodological inconsistencies, inconsistent labeling, and limited generalizability. This review establishes a comprehensive overview of the literature essential for developing robust BCI applications for cybersickness in user-aware adaptive systems.
