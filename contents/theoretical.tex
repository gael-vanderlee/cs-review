\subsection{The Etiology of Cybersickness}
The precise etiology of cybersickness remains a subject of investigation, with several theories proposed to explain its multifaceted nature.
These theories are not always mutually exclusive and often provide complementary perspectives on the phenomenon.

The most dominant framework is the \textit{Sensory Conflict Theory}, which posits that cybersickness arises from a discrepancy between information received from different sensory channels, particularly the visual, vestibular, and proprioceptive systems \cite{davisSystematicReviewCybersickness2014, zhangMotionSicknessCurrent2016}.
Several specific hypotheses branch from this core theory.
The \textit{Vection conflict hypothesis} is more specific and states that the visual system needs to perceive vection while the vestibular and proprioceptive systems report a stationary or incongruent physical state to experience cybersickness \cite{hettingerVectionSimulatorSickness1990}.
The \textit{Subjective Vertical Mismatch Theory} suggests a conflict between the perceived gravitational vertical and the one predicted by the brain based on visual cues \cite{blesMotionSicknessOnly1998}.
The \textit{Rest Frame Conflict Hypothesis} attributes sickness to ambiguity in identifying a stable, stationary frame of reference within the environment \cite{protheroUnifiedApproachPresence2003}. 
Furthermore, the \textit{Poison Theory} offers an evolutionary explanation, proposing that the brain misinterprets the sensory mismatch as a sign of neurotoxin ingestion, triggering a defensive response that includes nausea and vomiting \cite{treismanMotionSicknessEvolutionary1977}. % et pareil aussi pour cette citaiton

A second prominent framework is the \textit{Postural Instability Theory}.
This theory proposes that cybersickness is not a direct result of sensory mismatch, but rather a consequence of a prolonged inability to maintain stable postural control \cite{cortesEEGbasedExperimentVR2023a}.
According to this view, the unfamiliar sensory dynamics of a virtual environment challenge the user's postural control system; the resulting instability, which can be measured objectively, precedes and predicts the subjective experience of sickness.

Other theories focus on more specific physiological mechanisms.
The \textit{Vergence-Accommodation Conflict} is a factor unique to stereoscopic displays like HMDs.
It describes the mismatch between the distance at which the eyes converge to fuse a stereoscopic image and the fixed focal distance of the device's screen, leading to eyestrain and discomfort \cite{kramidaResolvingVergenceAccommodationConflict2016}. % pareil pour la citation
The \textit{Eye Movement Theory} suggests that sickness is caused not by the sensory conflict itself, but by the specific patterns of eye movements (e.g., nystagmus, torsion) that are generated by the vestibular system in an attempt to compensate for the perceived motion \cite{ebenholtzMotionSicknessOculomotor1992}. 

More recently, these concepts have been integrated within a \textit{Predictive Coding Framework} \cite{nurnbergerMismatchVisualVestibularInformation2021}.
This view re-contextualizes sensory conflict as a failure of predictive processing.
The brain is conceptualized as a prediction engine, constantly generating models of the world to anticipate sensory inputs.
When there is a persistent and unresolvable prediction error, a large discrepancy between expected and actual sensory signals, the brain fails to update its internal model.
This failure to reconcile the conflict is hypothesized to manifest as the aversive experience of cybersickness.

\subsection{Neuroimaging Modalities for Cybersickness Research}
While cybersickness assessment has traditionally relied on subjective measures such as questionnaires, the need for objective, real-time metrics has driven researchers toward neuroimaging approaches to measure corresponding brain activity.
Electroencephalography (EEG) is the most widely adopted modality in the reviewed literature.
Its principal advantages are its excellent temporal resolution, allowing for the tracking of rapid changes in neural dynamics, its portability, which facilitates studies in mobile and interactive VR paradigms \cite{cortesEEGbasedExperimentVR2023a, krokosQuantifyingVRCybersickness2022}, as well as its relative affordability.
Functional Near-Infrared Spectroscopy (fNIRS) is an emerging optical imaging technique that measures hemodynamic changes in cortical blood flow.
It offers a practical balance between temporal and spatial resolution and is notably more robust to motion artifacts than Functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI), making it well-suited for cybersickness research involving movement \cite{yamamuraHemodynamicVRAdaptingUsers2021a, zhangAnalysisMotionSickness2020}.
Finally, fMRI provides unparalleled spatial resolution, enabling the precise localization of activity in both cortical and subcortical structures.
However, it also has a lower temporal resolution and requires the subject to be immobile, which can be problematic for the study of cybersickness.
Despite these limitations, fMRI has been instrumental in identifying the core brain networks underlying nausea and sensory integration, such as the insular and cingulate cortices \cite{napadowBrainCircuitryUnderlying2013, toschiMotionSicknessIncreases2017}.
