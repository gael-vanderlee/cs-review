The methodological challenges documented in \autoref{sec:discussion} translate into six concrete directions for future work: standardised reporting and open data, causal mechanisms, generalisation and ecological validation, sex- and gender-stratified design, objective behavioural markers, and longitudinal characterisation of adaptation and recovery.

First, the field needs a standardised reporting set and shared infrastructure.
For classification and prediction studies, a minimum reporting set follows from the gaps documented in \autoref{sec:discussion}: a primary discriminative metric (AUC or balanced accuracy) reported with a 95\% confidence interval; sensitivity and specificity at a stated operating point; the class balance of the test fold; the numeric SSQ or FMS cutoff that converts continuous ratings into a label; a validation scheme that respects subject identity (leave-one-subject-out or held-out-subject k-fold); and a comparison against a trivial baseline (majority class, chance) and at least one non-neural feature baseline.
For studies that quantify in-exposure neural change, the baseline used to anchor the contrast should be matched to the exposure condition on visual input, oculomotor state, and vigilance: an eyes-open resting state with the head-mounted display on and a static neutral scene \cite{liRestingstateEEGVestibular2023}, a pre-stimulus segment immediately preceding stimulus onset \cite{wuInhibitionrelatedN2P32020}, or a sham non-nauseogenic VR exposure of matched duration \cite{takeuchiModulationExcitabilityTemporoparietal2018}; the choice should be reported with its rationale.
For EEG, the reference electrode and notch-filter frequency should be stated (currently absent from 56\% and 77\% of the 77 EEG corpus studies, respectively); both are required for reproducibility and meta-analytic re-pooling.
These reporting standards complement, rather than replace, the call for shared infrastructure: community-curated benchmark datasets that span passive (roller-coaster) and active (navigation) experiences, with multimodal recordings (EEG, EOG, ECG, head motion, sway, eye tracking) and well-defined subjective labels.
A small number of corpus papers have already released public datasets \cite{demirelSubjectivityContinuousCybersickness2025, kimDeepCybersicknessPredictor2019, dasdemirClassificationEmotionalImmersive2023}; releasing analysis code and pre-trained models alongside the data would further support replication and cumulative analysis.

Second, the field should move beyond correlation toward causal mechanisms.
The literature has documented associations between specific neural patterns and cybersickness across multiple studies, but the causal status of these associations remains largely unexamined.
Non-invasive brain stimulation offers a direct test: transcranial alternating-current stimulation can target specific oscillations (e.g., alpha or theta), and existing corpus studies that combine stimulation with VR exposure \cite{takeuchiModulationExcitabilityTemporoparietal2018, bergerInfluencePlaceboTDCS2024} provide a methodological template for asking whether modulating a candidate neuromarker changes sickness severity.
Coupled with computational models of multisensory integration and predictive coding, interventional designs can begin to distinguish neural features that drive the cybersickness experience from features that merely accompany it.

Third, models built for cybersickness must generalise and must be validated in ecologically valid contexts.
In the four corpus studies that report both within- and cross-subject accuracy, cross-subject performance is lower in each case, and only one of 38 classification studies reports a leave-one-subject-out evaluation; transfer-learning and domain-adaptation approaches that explicitly model inter-subject and inter-session variability are necessary if brain-directed methods are to operate without per-user calibration.
Validation should also move beyond the laboratory: at-home, naturalistic paradigms such as the in-home gaming setup of \cite{moinnereauInstrumentingVirtualReality2022} are needed to assess whether laboratory neuromarkers survive the noise of consumer-grade hardware and uncontrolled environments.

Fourth, sex and gender should be analysed as a primary factor.
Of the 92 corpus studies, 11 used male-only samples, 15 did not report sex composition, and only three -- all from a single laboratory, and therefore not independently replicated -- reported higher cybersickness in women \cite{bergerSexDifferencesUser2021, bergerInfluencePlaceboTDCS2024, bergerManipulatingCybersicknessVirtual2025}.
The broader literature on sex differences in cybersickness is mixed: \cite{kellyGenderDifferencesCybersickness2023} argues that reported effects are modest in magnitude and plausibly mediated by interpupillary-distance fit, prior gaming experience, and questionnaire framing; \cite{kourtesisCybersicknessCognitionMotor2023} reports that male--female differences disappear when prior gaming experience is included as a covariate; and \cite{lawsonAttentionMilitaryCommercial2023} caution that the claim of greater female susceptibility is overstated in the simulation literature.
Sex-stratified outcomes (band-power changes, connectivity, classification metrics) should be reported even when single-study samples are underpowered for inferential testing, so that subsequent meta-analytic re-pooling is possible.
Interpupillary-distance setting and gaming experience should be logged as covariates, both because they confound sex effects and because they connect to the hardware effects documented in \autoref{sec:confounders}.
Adequately powered designs --- pooled multi-site cohorts or longitudinal within-subject designs --- are needed before any individual study can credibly settle the magnitude or moderators of a sex effect on cybersickness.

Fifth, the field should develop objective behavioural markers to supplement self-report.
The candidate markers introduced in \autoref{sec:discussion} are already being collected by 16 of the 92 corpus studies, but typically as covariates rather than as candidate ground truth.
Postural sway is the strongest theoretically anchored candidate: studies should report a coupling estimate between pre-symptomatic sway dynamics and subsequent self-reported sickness, alongside the protocol metadata that this estimate depends on (stance, surface, sampling rate).
Pupillometry can supplement self-report subject to arousal and cognitive-load confounds, and requires controls on ambient luminance and on task demand.
Heart-rate variability, electrodermal activity, and head-motion dynamics are further candidates whose corpus-level predictive value is not yet quantified; multi-modal integration that fuses behavioural, autonomic, and neural signals offers a more holistic state estimate than any single modality.
Adopting these markers as outcome variables, rather than as covariates, is the precondition for shifting cybersickness research toward objective ground truth.

Finally, the field should adopt longitudinal study designs to characterise adaptation and recovery.
The corpus is dominated by single-session experiments, leaving the neurodynamics by which users adapt to VR (``getting one's VR legs'') largely uncharacterised, and the single corpus recovery study \cite{wooRecoveryTimeVR2023} is insufficient to support strong claims about residual neural state.
Multi-session within-subject designs are needed to track how the EEG signatures identified in this review evolve with repeated exposure, and to map the dissipation of post-exposure signatures onto behaviour.
